# Introduction Notes

## Driving Question

Is there a gap or weakness in Reformation Protestantism that contributes to spiritually shallow Christian living?

## Felt Problems / Diagnostic Context

- Many modern evangelicals feel that something is missing in their spiritual formation.
- The author himself experienced difficulty articulating what meaningful Christian growth practically looked like beyond a generic "quiet time."
- Evangelicalism may possess an underdeveloped practical theology of sanctification in common practice, even where doctrinal orthodoxy remains strong.

## Diagnostic Support

- Richard Lovelace's concept of the "sanctification gap" continues to resonate decades later.
- Protestant traditions may have misplaced historic models of spiritual growth in favor of activism, busyness, or reductionistic evangelistic emphasis.

## Critique of Existing Solutions

- Many modern attempts to address the spiritual formation gap do not consistently align with Reformation theological commitments.
- Some proposed solutions implicitly assume Protestantism itself lacks sufficient resources for deep spiritual formation.

## Major Claims

- Evangelical Protestantism possesses a rich biblical and historical tradition of spiritual formation that is often neglected or forgotten.
- The Reformed tradition contains substantial theological resources for cultivating deep Christian piety and growth.

## Meta Thesis / Book Purpose

This book seeks to recover and commend a distinctly Reformed Protestant vision of spiritual formation grounded in the theological commitments that originally motivated the Reformation.

## Definitions

- "early modern" = 1500-1800
- Protestant Reformers = Lutheran + Reformed + Radical
- Puritans = group of sixteenth and seventeenth century English Protestants who wanted to bring the Church of England into closer alignment with the Reformed tradition, from ~1550 to ~1700
